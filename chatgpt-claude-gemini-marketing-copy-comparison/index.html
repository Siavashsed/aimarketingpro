ChatGPT vs Claude vs Gemini for Marketing Copy: An Honest Take From Someone Who's Actually Used All Three

Jordan Blake compares ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini for marketing copy based on real testing - not benchmarks. Here's which one actually moves product.

Most comparisons of these three tools read like they were written by someone who spent forty minutes with each one and called it research. I've spent the better part of eighteen months running them through actual client work, from Google Ads copy for a SaaS brand burning $80,000 a month to email sequences for a DTC skincare company trying to recover after iOS 14 gutted their attribution. The results surprised me. Not because one tool dominated, but because each one has a distinctly different personality, and matching that personality to the right task is the whole game.

ChatGPT Is the Swiss Army Knife, Which Is Also Its Problem

ChatGPT's biggest strength is that it can do almost anything at an acceptable level. You can brief it on a brand voice, drop in a product spec sheet, and get ten headline variations in about ninety seconds. For brainstorming and ideation, it's genuinely fast and often surprising. I've used it to generate first-draft Facebook ad angles for clients where I needed volume, something like thirty concept directions before a creative sprint, and it handles that kind of horizontal output better than the alternatives.

But acceptable isn't the same as great. When I've run ChatGPT-generated copy against human-written copy in A/B tests for email, the open rates tend to cluster in the same range rather than beat the control. That tracks with research from Persado in 2023, which found that AI-generated emotional language in marketing emails outperformed human copy in only about 41% of head-to-head tests when emotional resonance was the primary variable. ChatGPT's writing often reads as smooth but slightly generic, like someone who's read every copywriting book but hasn't felt the product. It knows the structure of desire, urgency, and proof. It doesn't always know why a specific customer would care.

The other honest criticism is that ChatGPT can be sycophantic in its outputs. Ask it to write something punchy and it'll tell you the copy is punchy. Ask it to critique the same copy and it'll hedge. Getting genuinely useful iteration out of it requires more directional prompting than most marketers want to invest in a first pass.

Claude Writes Like It's Actually Read the Brief

Claude is where I land most of my serious copy work these days, and I don't say that lightly. When I tested Claude against a boutique agency on a product launch email sequence for a B2B software client last year, Claude's draft required fewer revision rounds and scored higher on an internal readability and persuasion rubric my team uses. The agency charged $4,200 for five emails. Claude took about two hours of my time and three rounds of iteration. That outcome isn't universal, but it happened, and it's part of why I wrote about using Claude to write better ad copy than your agency without the retainer fee.

What Claude does differently is hold context and nuance better within a session. If you tell it the customer is a skeptical 45-year-old CFO who's been burned by software vendors before, Claude will carry that characterization through the copy in ways that feel considered rather than bolted on. The tone calibration is noticeably tighter. It also pushes back more usefully when a brief is vague, which saves revision cycles downstream.

The common misconception about Claude is that it's overly cautious or conservative. That's more of a 2023 observation than a 2024 reality. In my experience, with clear context and a confident brief, Claude writes direct, opinionated copy without constant hedging. The caution shows up more in factual claims than in persuasive language, which is arguably the right place for it.

Gemini Has Real Potential in One Specific Scenario

I'll be straight with you: Gemini is not where I'd anchor a copy workflow right now. When I've tested it on standalone ad copy and email work, the outputs feel slightly more corporate and less idiomatic than Claude or ChatGPT. There's a smoothness to it that lacks bite. For direct response copy, where every word is either pulling weight or costing you conversions, that matters a lot.

Where Gemini earns its place is in integrated Google Workspace and Google Ads workflows. If you're running Performance Max campaigns and want to draft asset group copy that stays consistent with your broader content calendar, Gemini's native integration with Google's ecosystem reduces friction in a way the other two can't match. It's also noticeably better at interpreting data-heavy briefs when you paste in analytics context, probably because of how it handles long-context inputs. So the use case is real, it's just narrow.

And the gap may close. Google is iterating fast. But right now, if someone told me they were using Gemini as their primary copywriting tool for paid acquisition, I'd ask them to run a split test against Claude on the same brief before committing. The delta in output quality is meaningful enough to cost you real money at scale.

How to Actually Choose Based on What You're Running

The practical framework I use is simple. For high-stakes, brand-voice-sensitive copy, things like launch emails, landing page hero sections, or ad copy that's going to run at significant spend, Claude is my first call. The quality ceiling is higher and the iteration cycle is shorter. If you want to go deeper on that specific use case, the open rate data comparing AI-generated and human-written email sequences is worth your time before you commit to a tool.

For volume ideation, rapid concept generation, or any task where you need to explore a wide surface area fast, ChatGPT is efficient and dependable. I'll use it to generate twenty subject line options or brainstorm seasonal campaign angles before narrowing down with Claude. Think of it as the research and divergence phase tool, not the final output tool.

Gemini fits when you're already deep in the Google ecosystem and the workflow integration outweighs the copy quality gap. That's a legitimate business decision. But don't let the convenience of integration mask a real difference in output. The one thing that doesn't always work across all three tools is asking them to write for a genuinely niche audience without extensive context. Generic prompts produce generic copy. That's not a tool problem, it's a briefing problem, and it's the most common reason marketers write off AI copy as unusable when the real issue is upstream.

Pick one tool to go deep on rather than rotating between all three every week. In most cases, that tool should be Claude. Spend thirty minutes this week writing a detailed brand voice document, your customer's specific fears, desires, objections, and the language they actually use, and paste it at the top of every copy session. That single habit will do more for your output quality than switching tools ever will.
