Pricing Psychology: Why $97 Beats $99 in Most DTC Markets

By Siavash Sadighi

Photo by Tamanna Rumee / Pexels

Everyone in ecommerce knows charm pricing works. You end a price in 9, customers perceive it as cheaper, and conversions go up. That's the received wisdom, anyway. But here's what most people running Shopify stores don't stop to question: not all charm prices are equal. The difference between $97 and $99 is two dollars on paper. In practice, it can be the difference between a price that feels like a deal and one that feels like a trap.

My position is simple: in most DTC markets, especially anything priced between $50 and $300, ending in 7 outperforms ending in 9. Not always by a massive margin, but consistently enough that it should be the default starting point for any brand doing serious conversion work. The reason isn't magic. It's a combination of pattern recognition, competitive saturation, and how trust gets built before someone clicks Add to Cart.

Why .99 Pricing Has Become a Trust Signal for the Wrong Kind of Store

There's a reason dollar stores, fast food value menus, and clearance aisles all love the .99. It's become culturally associated with rock-bottom pricing, high-volume commodities, and frankly, brands that don't take themselves too seriously. For a DTC brand trying to position itself above Amazon and mass retail, that association is a liability. When someone lands on a premium skincare product priced at $49.99, there's a subtle cognitive friction that happens. The product page is saying 'quality.' The price ending is saying 'bargain bin.'

Research from 2014 by Pricing Advisor and consumer behavior researcher Eric Anderson found that .99 endings underperform compared to other charm price endings when the product is positioned as aspirational or higher quality. The .99 works hardest in genuinely price-sensitive, low-trust environments. In DTC, you're usually trying to build the opposite of that.

I've seen this pattern play out across multiple brand audits. One wellness brand I worked with was pricing a $64.99 supplement stack and couldn't figure out why their add-to-cart rate was soft despite solid creative. We shifted to $67 and then tested $63 as a round number. The $67 outperformed both. The .99 had been quietly undermining the premium positioning they'd spent real money building through branding and photography.

What Makes 7 Feel Different to the Human Brain

The psychology here isn't complicated, but it is interesting. Seven is considered the least 'round' single digit in most Western cognitive frameworks. Studies on numerical cognition suggest people associate odd, non-round numbers with precision and specificity. When a brand prices something at $97, the implied message is: we calculated this carefully. It doesn't feel arbitrary the way $99 does, and it doesn't feel rounded-up the way $100 does.

There's also a scarcity-of-use effect. Because .97 is so much rarer in retail environments than .99 or .95, it registers as slightly unusual. And unusual, in the right context, means memorable. The price sticks. That might sound like a minor thing, but when someone's comparison-shopping across three or four tabs, being the price they actually remember matters.

And there's the gap perception. The psychological distance from $99 to $100 is well-documented. But $97 to $100 feels like a bigger gap even though it's only $2 more. Customers round to the nearest ten, not the nearest dollar. So $97 reads closer to 'somewhere in the $90s' while $99 sits uncomfortably close to $100 in perceived value. That two-dollar difference in actual price represents a larger perceptual gap in the customer's mind.

Where This Breaks Down and When to Ignore It

I want to be honest about where this logic doesn't hold. In genuinely price-war categories, like phone cases, basic apparel basics, or commodity supplements sold primarily through paid acquisition with thin margins, the .99 convention is so entrenched that deviating from it can hurt conversions. Customers in those categories have been trained to compare prices at the cent level. Breaking the pattern there just looks like a pricing error.

There's also a legitimate debate about whether ending in 5 sometimes beats both. A $95 or $145 price point reads clean and confident. Some luxury-adjacent brands do very well dropping charm pricing entirely and going with round numbers. If your brand sits in the $200 to $500 range and your customer is the kind of person who'd buy at a boutique, round pricing might signal more confidence than any charm ending.

The common misconception I'd push back on hardest is the idea that any odd-number ending is better than even. That's not the finding. It's specifically that in mid-market DTC, the .97 ending outperforms .99 on products where quality perception matters. If you're selling a $12 product, none of this matters much. But if you're at $87, $127, or $197, the ending carries real weight. Make sure your product page isn't already undermining conversion before the price even registers, because a better price ending won't save a page with structural trust problems.

How to Test This Without Wasting Three Months

When I tested .97 versus .99 endings on a home goods brand doing around $180k per month in revenue, we ran a structured A/B test across their top five SKUs over six weeks using Intelligems. The .97 variants won on conversion rate in four out of five SKUs, with an average lift of around 3.2%. That's not earth-shattering, but compounded across a full catalog it moved the needle on monthly revenue by a measurable amount without changing a single creative asset.

The practical approach is to pick your three to five highest-traffic product pages, shift the price endings from .99 to .97, and let it run for at least four weeks with enough traffic to hit statistical significance. Don't test this on your lowest-price SKUs first. Test where the price decision carries real weight, typically your $60 to $200 range. That's where pricing psychology has the most room to operate.

Also, don't test this in isolation from the rest of the page. In my work with DTC brands on conversion optimization, the price ending is almost never the only variable. But it's one of the fastest to change, requires no design work, and costs nothing to implement. It's the kind of test that takes 20 minutes to set up and can inform your entire pricing architecture going forward. Start there, see what your data says, and build from it.

Pick one product page today, ideally a mid-range SKU between $60 and $200, and change the price ending from .99 to .97. Set a reminder to check conversion data in 30 days. You don't need a complex multivariate test to start learning. The brands I've seen treat pricing as a live, testable variable rather than a set-and-forget decision consistently find margin and conversion improvements that purely creative-focused teams miss entirely. Two dollars isn't just two dollars. Sometimes it's the difference between a price that closes and one that hesitates.
