We Spent a Semester Building a Self-Extending Marketing Agent With Queen's University Students. Here Is What Broke.

A behind-the-scenes build diary: how the Kavalsia team partnered with QMIND AI students at Queen's University to build an autonomous, self-extending marketing agent, what failed, and what shipped.

JC
Jordan Cole December 05, 2025 · 3 min read

In the early autumn of 2025 we had a problem that no amount of hiring was going to solve. Across the brands we run, the number of signals worth acting on every day had quietly outgrown the number of humans who could act on them. Pixels broke at 2am. A campaign hit its window for an hour and we found out the next morning. The honest answer was not more marketers, it was a different kind of teammate. So we did something we had been circling for a year. We sat down with a group of AI students from QMIND, the design team at Queen's University, and started building one.

What follows is the build diary. Not the polished case study, the actual sequence of things that worked, things that embarrassed us, and the few decisions that turned out to matter more than all the others.

The brief: a teammate, not a chatbot

We were deliberate about the framing from day one, because framing decides architecture.

We did not want a chatbot bolted onto a dashboard. We wanted something closer to a colleague: a system that reads every signal across ads, store, email, CRM and content, decides what to do, and then actually does it, with guardrails. The QMIND students pushed back hard on the word autonomous in the first week, and they were right to. Autonomy without a rollback trail is just an expensive way to make mistakes faster. That tension, between moving on its own and never moving irreversibly, became the spine of the whole project.

The first thing that broke: the data was lying

Our first prototype made confident decisions on garbage.

The browser-side tracking we had trusted for years was dropping a meaningful fraction of events, and the agent, reasoning faithfully on incomplete data, produced faithful nonsense. The students spent most of October not on the model at all but on a signal layer: pulling live data straight from each platform, normalizing it into one shape, and recovering the conversions that client-side pixels quietly lose. The lesson was humbling and it has held up. The reasoning is never the bottleneck. The bottleneck is whether the inputs are true. We rebuilt the bottom of the stack before we let the top of it touch a budget.

The decision that mattered most: let it write its own tools

Halfway through, we hit the wall every integration project hits. There were more platforms to connect than we could ever hand-code.

One of the Queen's students proposed the idea that ended up defining the system: instead of pre-building every connector, give the agent the ability to write its own integration when it encounters a tool that does not exist yet, and plug itself in. It sounded reckless. In a sandbox, with spend ceilings and a human approval gate, it was the opposite. It meant the system stopped being a fixed set of capabilities and became something that extends itself. That single property, self-extension, is the difference between a tool you outgrow and a teammate that grows with you.

What we got wrong about models

We assumed one frontier model would do everything. It would not, and not for the reason we expected.

The expensive model was brilliant at planning and useless economics for bulk work. The cheaper model was fast and shallow. The fix was not picking a winner, it was routing: an ensemble where each task goes to the model that fits it, planning to one, high-volume reasoning to another. The students built the router in a weekend and it cut our cost per decision by more than half while raising quality, because the smart model stopped wasting its attention on work that did not need it. If you take one technical idea from this piece, take that one. Stop asking which model. Start asking which model for which task.

What shipped, and what it became

By the end of the semester we had something we trusted enough to point at real accounts.

It senses across every connected platform, reasons with a multi-model ensemble, acts inside guardrails with a full rollback trail, and remembers every account, decision and result so it never starts from zero. The students went back to campus. The system stayed, kept extending itself, and grew into the operating layer behind every brand we run. It has a name now, Nexus, and the version live today is several generations past the one we built that autumn. But the bones are the ones a handful of QMIND students argued us into: true data first, self-extension, model routing, and never a move you cannot undo.

The story people want from an AI build is a clean arc from idea to launch. The real one is messier and more useful. A group of students at Queen's spent a semester making us throw out our assumptions one at a time, and the agent that survived that process is better for every assumption it killed. If you are building your own, do not start with the model. Start with whether your data is true, decide early what the system is never allowed to do without you, and let it write the parts of itself you would otherwise spend years hand-coding.
